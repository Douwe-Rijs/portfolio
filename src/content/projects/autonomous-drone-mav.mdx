## Overview

A team of eight engineers built a fully autonomous flight stack for a **Parrot
Bebop 2** micro air vehicle (MAV) as part of TU Delft's AE4317 course. The drone
had to explore the **CyberZoo** test arena, avoid the obstacles placed in it, and
— for bonus points — fly through a racing gate. The hard constraint: everything
had to run **on the drone's own ARM processor**, using only its front and bottom
cameras. No external motion capture, no off-board compute.

### The platform

The Bebop is small, light and inherently safe to fly indoors — but that agility
comes at the cost of sensing and compute. Two cameras (one forward, one down) and
a modest onboard processor were the entire perception budget. Every algorithm we
shipped had to share that processor with the flight controller and still leave
headroom for stable flight.

</Split>

## The problem

MAVs trade sensing and compute for agility and inherent safety — they are small
and light, which makes them safe to fly indoors, but leaves very little
processing headroom. Our target was to keep perception running at roughly **10
frames per second** onboard; anything slower starves the flight controller's
update loop and produces erratic, unsafe motion. Every algorithm therefore had
to earn its place against a strict latency budget while remaining robust to the
CyberZoo's changing lighting, cluttered background and motion blur.

## Approach & design decisions

We deliberately started simple and added complexity only where it paid off.

We first **prototyped a machine-learning route**: a PyTorch CNN classifier
reached **98% validation accuracy** across four classes (nothing / pole / gate /
both), but a YOLO detector reached only **13.9% mAP@50** and would have needed
far more hand-annotated data to become reliable. Given the annotation effort and
the onboard compute cost, we **chose not to deploy ML on the drone** and instead
invested in a layered **classical-vision** stack:

- An **orange-pole avoider** as a baseline obstacle detector and a way to
  validate the Paparazzi autopilot integration.
- A **multi-layer obstacle detector** that splits each frame into 22 columns and
  fuses five cues — sparse optical flow, vertical-line (Hough) edges, an
  orange-pole mask, a green-ground disruption score, and a textureless-region
  detector — into a single per-column danger map plus a ranked set of safe gaps.
- A **gate detector** built on blue-segment (HSV/BGR) masking, morphological
  cleanup and a geometric pairing filter that rejects mismatched gate posts.
- A **bottom-camera border detector** to keep the drone inside the arena without
  relying on external positioning.
- An **optical-flow velocity estimator** to move toward OptiTrack-free flight.

## Technical architecture

The deployed pipeline is organised as a **state machine** over the perception
modules. A _Normal_ state continuously evaluates the obstacle detectors; when the
gate is seen for enough consecutive frames the controller switches to _Gate
Approach_, flying orthogonally toward the gate centre, then to _Gate Coast_ to
carry through once the gate leaves the field of view. A detected boundary
overrides everything and triggers _Avoid Border_. Control outputs are smoothed
with a moving-average window to damp erratic behaviour.

## Engineering for the compute budget

The defining challenge was latency. The full fused pipeline ran at just **0.6
FPS** on the Bebop — far too slow, and exactly the cause of the wobbling we saw
in early flight tests. We responded on two fronts: profile-and-optimise, and
prefer the simplest module that is robust enough.

<PullQuote cite="Project retrospective">
  The full fused pipeline ran at 0.6 FPS. Profiling — not intuition — found the one line that was
  costing us the flight.
</PullQuote>

The clearest win was the velocity estimator. Its first implementation took
**300 ms per frame**. Primitive on-drone profiling pinpointed a single culprit —
OpenCV's `goodFeaturesToTrack` at 250 ms — which we replaced with the **FAST**
feature detector and a hard cap on tracked features. That cut the frame time to
**50–70 ms (~14 FPS)**, roughly a five-fold speed-up. For the competition itself
we deployed the **bottom-camera-only avoider** (8.6 FPS), the most robust
real-time option we had.

## Results

- **CNN classifier:** 98% validation accuracy (prototype; not deployed onboard).
- **Velocity estimator:** 300 → ~55 ms per frame, ~5× faster, ~14 FPS.
- **Deployed perception** ran at **8–11 FPS** on the drone's processor, meeting
  the real-time target.
- The **gate detector** reliably localised the gate and rejected geometrically
  inconsistent post pairs.
- In the competition the drone flew autonomously for sustained stretches, though
  less far than we had hoped. We traced the shortfall to an **HSV colour mask
  calibrated on the centre of the frame**, which under-segmented the frame edges
  and produced phantom obstacles — a small calibration assumption with an
  outsized effect on field performance.

## Lessons learned

- **Compute budget is a first-class design constraint.** On embedded hardware
  the simplest robust solution routinely beats the most capable one.
- **Profile before optimising.** The 250 ms bottleneck was invisible until it
  was measured; intuition would have sent us optimising the wrong stage.
- **Calibration assumptions can dominate field results.** Centre-frame colour
  calibration quietly determined how far we flew.
- **Testing time is scarce and valuable.** With only a handful of CyberZoo slots,
  arriving prepared mattered more than any single algorithmic improvement.
