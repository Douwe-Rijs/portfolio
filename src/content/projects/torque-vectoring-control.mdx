## Overview

A double lane change — swerving around an obstacle and back — is one of the
harshest everyday manoeuvres a passenger car performs: it demands accurate
lateral path tracking and yaw stability _at the same time_, and steering alone
struggles to deliver both. This RO47017 group project designed and compared two
**torque-vectoring (TV)** controllers — a hand-tuned **PID** and a
speed-scheduled **LQR** — layered on a steering-PD baseline, on a 9-DOF
nonlinear Simulink vehicle model with Magic-Formula tires, parameterised as a
Tesla Model 3 Performance and a Toyota Camry.

## The problem

A steering PD loop fast enough to correct lateral position tends to overshoot
or lag the yaw response, especially as front-tire slip grows through the
chicane. Torque vectoring adds a second, independent actuator: applying
**differential torque** between left and right wheels generates a direct yaw
moment, decoupling yaw control from steering. The question was not whether TV
is possible, but **which controller structure buys what** — raw tracking,
comfort, robustness — and at what actuator cost.

## Method

Both TV controllers act on the yaw-rate error against a filtered
bicycle-model reference (τ = 0.09 s), with ΔT saturated at ±1200 N·m and split
50/50 between axles:

- **PID-TV** — gains tuned manually on the Model 3
  (K<sub>p</sub> = 1300, K<sub>i</sub> = 60, K<sub>d</sub> = 30).
- **LQR-TV** — a linear bicycle model with state [β, r − r<sub>ref</sub>],
  Q = diag(15000, 15000), R = 10⁻⁴, gain-scheduled offline by solving the
  Riccati equation on a 5–130 km/h grid in 5 km/h steps.

All metrics are evaluated over the manoeuvre's active window at constant
entry speed.

## Results

Torque vectoring transformed the manoeuvre on the Model 3:

- **RMS lateral error:** 0.088 m (PD only) → **0.044 m** with PID-TV (−50%)
  and 0.056 m with LQR-TV.
- **RMS yaw-rate error:** 0.200 rad/s → **0.078** (PID-TV) / **0.073 rad/s**
  (LQR-TV) — a 61–63% reduction.
- **Peak lateral error:** 0.589 m → 0.266 m (PID-TV).

The two controllers trade different currencies. **PID-TV** achieves the best
raw lateral tracking and never saturates (peak |ΔT| = 812 N·m). **LQR-TV**
delivers the cleaner yaw response and a lower peak lateral acceleration
(7.62 vs 8.47 m/s²) — a more comfortable ride — but leans on the actuator,
sitting at the ±1200 N·m saturation limit for ~7% of the run.

The cross-vehicle test is the sharpest result. On the more understeering Camry
(K<sub>us</sub> ≈ 66% higher than the Model 3), the steering-only baseline
**cannot complete the manoeuvre at all** — it departs the track with a peak
lateral error of 30.5 m. With torque vectoring enabled, the same car finishes
under both controllers. TV is not an optimisation here; it is the difference
between completing the manoeuvre and leaving the road.

## Limitations

All results are for a single speed, friction level and manoeuvre severity, with
no sensor noise. The Camry ran with Model-3-tuned gains, so its numbers
understate what vehicle-specific tuning would achieve. The LQR gain schedule is
a discrete 5 km/h grid, and the reference filter's lag partially decouples
yaw-rate tracking from path-tracking quality.

## Lessons learned

- **A second actuator changes the problem class.** For the understeering
  vehicle, TV moved the outcome from _failure_ to _completion_ — no amount of
  steering-gain tuning does that.
- **PID vs LQR is a trade, not a ranking.** Best lateral tracking (PID) and
  best comfort/yaw response (LQR) live in different controllers; the "best"
  one depends on the vehicle and the objective.
- **Saturation is a design surface.** Relaxing the torque limit improved yaw
  error by 8.8% but _worsened_ lateral error by 6.5% — the controller operates
  on a trade-off boundary, not below it.
- **Always validate across plants.** The controller ranking flipped between the
  two vehicles; a single-vehicle conclusion would have been wrong.
