## Overview

Every pipe joint that goes into an offshore pipeline carries an identity:
supplier, specifications and — critically — a **pipe number**, stencilled or
chalked onto the steel. Matching physical pipes to the database today means a
person reading those markings by hand. This R&D project set out to automate it:
photograph the inside of a pipe, and have a vision pipeline **find and read the
pipe number** — robustly enough to verify the result against the company's
asset system.

## The problem

The input images are hostile to OCR. Text curves around the inside of a
cylinder, so every line is warped by **perspective and 3D curvature**. Photos
arrive in any **orientation**, glossy coatings throw **glare**, and chalked
numbers on dark steel offer almost no **contrast**. Some frames are simply
unusable.

<Split src={glareContrast} alt="A dim photo taken inside a dark pipe: stencilled text is barely legible through heavy glare and low contrast, with a torch reflection blowing out part of the frame." caption="A typical hard input: low contrast, glare and curved text on dark steel." reverse>

An early idea was to geometrically **unwrap the cylinder** — map the curved
text back to a flat plane and hand it to a standard OCR engine. It works on
paper, but it depends on a clean mask of the text region, and masks are exactly
what glare, orientation and contrast make difficult. The straightening approach
was parked, and the pipeline was reframed around a different idea: **isolate
the information first**, then read only that.

## Benchmarking the OCR landscape

Four open-source engines were put through detection and recognition tests on
real pipe imagery: **Tesseract**, **EasyOCR**, **PaddleOCR** and **TrOCR**.
Tesseract's classical stack needed binarisation and still underperformed;
EasyOCR detected reasonably but recognised poorly; transformer-based TrOCR was
"not so SOTA after all" for this domain. **PaddleOCR** was the clear winner on
both detection and recognition.

Winning the benchmark was not the same as solving the problem: end-to-end on
raw field images, even PaddleOCR **failed to produce the correct pipe number
almost every time**. The interesting work started here.

## From OCR to vision-language models

Training one classical OCR model to survive varying lighting, handwriting,
fonts and orientations was judged infeasible. **Vision-language models** are a
better fit for exactly that messiness, and while training one from scratch was
out of scope, **tuning and prompting** were not — with inference running on
Databricks cloud and the internal AI-lab server.

Untuned performance was lacking, so the next question was diagnostic: **what
separates a correct read from a wrong one?** Inspecting failures showed most of
the loss came from context — models grabbing neighbouring digits, chalk marks
and specification text. The accuracy was hiding behind **tighter crops**.

## Tighter crops: segmentation → oriented boxes

Segmentation masks confirmed the diagnosis — cropping tighter sharply reduced
faulty reads, and chalked digits could be reliably distinguished from
stencilled text. But per-pixel labelling is expensive, and masks risk cutting
away useful context. The pragmatic middle ground: **oriented bounding boxes**
(OBB), which focus the model on the ID while being far cheaper to label.

<Split src={yoloObb} alt="Black-and-white view inside a pipe with a green oriented bounding box drawn by a YOLO model around one stencilled text region, scored 0.30." caption="A YOLO OBB detector focusing the pipeline on the text that matters.">

With OBB preprocessing feeding tighter crops into a stronger recognition
model, exact-ID accuracy jumped to **almost 80%** — a step change from the
sub-1% end-to-end starting point. The lesson was blunt: in this pipeline,
**preprocessing bought more accuracy than model choice**.

</Split>

## The generalisation wall

Then came pipes from other projects. Colours, stencil layouts, text fonts,
chalk styles and pipe diameters all change between projects — and a detector
trained on one layout **quietly predicts the same relative location** on the
next, because it cannot actually read.

<FullBleed
  src={barossa}
  alt="Looking into a red-coated Barossa project pipe: white stencilled text arcs around the interior wall listing contractor, project, pipe size, heat number and coater."
  ratio="aspect-[16/9]"
  caption="A Barossa-project pipe: same task, different colours, stencil layout and diameter."
/>

That framed the real trade-off. **YOLO** is fast but layout-biased by
construction; a **VLM** can genuinely read a stencil but is slow, expensive and
easily confused by many lines. The middle path prototyped here: locate the ID
**without** understanding the stencil, using **CRAFT text and linking scores** —
which respond to _textness_ rather than colour or font, and worked across all
stencils and colours tested.

<Figure

  alt="CRAFT text-score heatmap of a pipe interior: curved strokes of stencilled characters glow red and yellow against a deep blue background, tracing the arc of the text."
  figNumber="2"
  caption="CRAFT text scores light up characters regardless of stencil, paint or pipe colour — a colour- and layout-agnostic signal for locating text."
/>

<Split src={arcFitting} alt="Diagnostic plot of a binarised pipe-text mask with blue radial lines and a fitted arc tracing the curvature of the stencilled text rows." caption="Fitting arcs through detected text to model the cylinder's curvature." reverse>

The text scores double as geometry: fitting **arcs** through them recovers the
text's curvature and direction, which is enough to build **one unwrapped text
map per project**. Locate the pipe number once on that map, and its relative
position pins the ID in every other photo from the same project — one manual
step per project instead of one per pipe.

</Split>

## Capturing the whole pipe at once

A regular camera can miss the ID entirely if it happens to face away from the
lens. A **360° camera** cannot — all text is always somewhere in the frame,
which also makes continuous recording of whole pipe stacks plausible. Drag
through a real capture from inside a pipe:

  alt="Equirectangular 360-degree capture from inside a rusted pipe, with stencilled project and specification text arcing across the lower half and daylight visible at both open ends"

On these panoramas an **immediate crop method** proved attractive: no YOLO, no
unwarping — detect text, crop **characters individually**, and simply index the
characters that form the ID (say, line 4, characters 10–14). Individual crops
also sidestep distortion and give the recogniser its best-case input.

## Results

- Exact-ID accuracy went from **~1% to almost 80%** — driven primarily by OBB
  preprocessing and a stronger recognition model, not by any single "better"
  OCR engine.
- **PaddleOCR** won the four-way benchmark on both detection and recognition;
  recognised IDs are cross-checked against the ship's asset database via an
  internal API.
- **CRAFT text/linking scores** located text reliably across every stencil,
  colour and pipe tested — the most generalisable component of the system.
- Honest caveats: accuracy is not 100% (overlapping text, similar characters),
  a wrong read can go unnoticed without database confirmation, and the mapping
  approach still needs **one manual localisation per project**.

## Lessons learned

- **Diagnose before you tune.** The jump from 30% to 80% came from asking what
  separated right from wrong answers — the fix was preprocessing, not a bigger
  model.
- **Benchmarks are not pipelines.** The best engine on curated tests still
  failed end-to-end; the gap _was_ the project.
- **Bias can be structural.** A detector that cannot read will always guess by
  layout; no amount of de-biasing changes what the architecture can express.
- **Change the sensor, change the problem.** 360° capture dissolved the
  "is the ID even in frame?" failure mode more cheaply than any model fix.
