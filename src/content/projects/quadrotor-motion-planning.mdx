## Overview

A four-person TU Delft project on motion planning for quadrotor delivery in
constrained disaster environments: after an earthquake or collapse, a drone
must carry supplies through rubble, doorways and half-blocked windows to reach
people a ground vehicle cannot. We combined **RRT and RRT\*** global planners
with a **dual-loop PID (DSLPID)** trajectory tracker on a simulated Crazyflie
quadrotor in PyBullet, and benchmarked systematically: _how narrow can the gap
get before planning fails?_

My part was the **experiment infrastructure**: a procedural environment
generator producing randomised walls with window-like openings of controllable
width and height, and the **results analysis** turning hundreds of runs into
the plots below.

## The problem

Sampling-based planners are probabilistically complete — given infinite
samples, they find a path if one exists. Disaster response doesn't have
infinite samples. **Narrow passages** are their known weakness: uniform random
sampling rarely lands useful points inside a small opening, so planning time
explodes exactly where the mission is hardest. We wanted the failure boundary
as a measured curve, not an anecdote.

## Approach

- **Planning space:** 3D position only, with obstacles padded by the drone's
  radius — a point-mass abstraction that keeps planning fast while the
  controller absorbs the dynamics.
- **RRT vs RRT\*:** identical sampling; RRT\* additionally rewires the tree
  within a search radius toward lower path cost.
- **Tracking:** each planned edge is discretised into 100 waypoints and fed to
  the DSLPID position/attitude cascade, running on full rigid-body dynamics in
  `gym-pybullet-drones`.
- **Benchmark:** randomised environments sweeping hole width, height and the
  number of walls; metrics are success rate and flight time on successful runs.

## What the data says

Three findings stand out from the gallery above:

**Success is a function of hole area — and shape.** Success rate climbs
roughly linearly with hole area, and the width × height heatmap shows width
matters more than height: the drone is wider than it is tall, so wide-short
openings pass where tall-narrow ones fail.

**RRT\* wins on path quality, not on reachability.** Both planners succeed at
comparable rates, but RRT\*'s rewiring yields smoother paths with fewer
waypoints — and roughly **half the flight time** of RRT on harder maps, with
far less sensitivity to hole size. RRT's flight times grow steeply as gaps
shrink; RRT\*'s barely move.

**Tight gaps cost time even when they work.** On successful runs, mean flight
time falls from ~9.5 s for the smallest holes to ~6.5 s for the largest — the
controller slows and corrects more in confined space.

## Limitations

Static environments only — a changed world means replanning from scratch. The
simulation assumes perfect state information (no sensor noise), sampling is
uniform (informed/targeted sampling was consciously left out to keep the
comparison general), and nearest-neighbour search is O(n) where a KD-tree
would cut it to O(log n).

## Lessons learned

- **Measure the failure boundary.** "RRT struggles in narrow passages" became a
  concrete, reproducible curve: below ~0.17 m² of opening, success drops under
  15%.
- **Asymptotic guarantees ≠ practical performance.** Probabilistic completeness
  says nothing about the sample budget a real mission can afford.
- **Geometry is a design input.** The width-vs-height asymmetry says: match the
  drone's cross-section to the openings you expect, or pay in success rate.
- **Separate planning from tracking.** The point-mass + padding + PID split kept
  each layer simple, at the cost of conservatism in genuinely tight spaces.
