## Overview

For the TU Delft Multidisciplinary Project (RO47007), a five-person team built an
autonomous greenhouse-monitoring robot for the fictitious client _Flora Nova_.
Built on the **MIRTE Master** platform, the robot maps the greenhouse with SLAM,
navigates autonomously between flower beds, detects **flowers and pests** with
an onboard neural network, reads **per-bed climate data** via AprilTags, and
streams everything into a live web-based **digital twin** — with tele-operation
override always one click away.

## The problem

Manual greenhouse monitoring is slow, inconsistent and reactive: by the time a
pest infestation is noticed on a scheduled round, it has often spread. The
client asked for a robot that monitors plant growth and environmental
conditions **continuously**, navigates safely among narrow paths, plants and
people, and makes its own state fully transparent to staff — remotely via a
dashboard, and on the spot via lights and speech.

## Architecture

The system is a ROS 2 node graph on top of the MIRTE Master. A custom
**state machine** (built on `yasmin`) orchestrates everything: it dispatches
routes to a custom global planner feeding **Nav2**, triggers the camera-arm
scanning poses on arrival, monitors battery and faults, and owns the
return-to-dock behaviour. Perception runs in parallel — a **YOLOv8** detector
(single inference per frame for both flowers and bugs), an **AprilTag** reader
for per-bed climate lookup, and an aggregator that deduplicates detections into
per-bed summaries for the digital twin.

Design decisions leaned deliberately on proven components: Nav2 and the
platform's SLAM stack were reused rather than rebuilt ("re-developing this
would consume the majority of the project budget without outperforming existing
solutions"), while the mission logic, planner, perception pipeline and
interface — the parts specific to this problem — were custom.

## Seeing the flowers

The gripper camera feeds a YOLOv8 model that detects tulips (by colour) and
bugs in the same pass. Detections are aggregated with an IoU threshold of 0.45
and centre-pixel analysis to stop one flower being counted five times as the
robot drives past.

Tuning mattered more than architecture here: the confidence threshold sits low
(0.25) to survive poor camera conditions and odd flower poses, the detector was
throttled so it stopped starving the rest of the compute, and the robot's
driving speed was lowered so hidden bugs still get enough frames to be caught.

## The digital twin

Everything the robot knows lands in one browser dashboard: the live SLAM map
and robot pose, camera feeds, per-bed flower histograms with heights and
colours, pest flags, climate history for every scanned AprilTag, battery,
system log and a 3D view. Operators can drag bed markers onto the map, dispatch
the robot to any bed with one click, or take direct manual control.

## Validation

Eight structured tests covered teleoperation, SLAM, navigation, both detection
pipelines, the UI, the HMI hardware and obstacle avoidance:

- **SLAM (T2):** a full traversal of the ten-row arena on real hardware, with
  loop closure, produced an occupancy grid closely matching the physical layout.
- **Detections (T4/T5):** reliable flower and bug detection within a single
  scanning pass; five unique AprilTags identified with all five climate
  readings correctly displayed.
- **Obstacle avoidance (T8):** the robot reliably stopped for people walking
  into its path and replanned around static obstacles, fusing 2D lidar with
  depth-camera data — with a known edge case for low-lying objects below the
  lidar's scan height.
- **Navigation (T3):** passed in simulation; on the real robot, full autonomous
  route execution was blocked by an integration error — the honest reason MR4
  is scored _partially met_.

The final tally: **seven of eight mandatory requirements met**, the eighth
partially, plus most optional ones. One hardware war story: the LED strip
short-circuited and took the onboard microcontroller with it — LED state
signalling works in software, but demo day ran on speech audio alone.

## Lessons learned

- **Integrate early, not at the end.** The team's biggest design change was
  abandoning a big-bang integration in favour of developing features that work
  independently in parallel — the one that didn't fully merge (real-robot Nav2
  execution) is exactly where the remaining gap lives.
- **Reuse buys you the interesting problems.** Standing on Nav2, SLAM Toolbox
  and apriltag_ros left the budget for the state machine, perception tuning and
  a digital twin that actually feels like a product.
- **Transparency is a feature, not a report chapter.** The dashboard, LEDs and
  speech weren't polish — they were what made a robot among people acceptable.
- **Hardware fails on its own schedule.** A short circuit two weeks before demo
  day is a risk model, not an anecdote.
