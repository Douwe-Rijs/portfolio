## Overview

Daratumumab (Darzalex), a monoclonal antibody used against multiple myeloma, is
injected subcutaneously: 15–20 mL pushed by hand over **3–5 minutes of
continuous pressure**. Nurses administering it several times a day report
serious strain in hand, wrist and forearm. For our Bachelor End Project at
TU Delft — in collaboration with **Reinier de Graaf Hospital** — our
four-person team designed, built and validated a device that takes that load
off the caregiver entirely: a 3D-printed **Constant Force Injector (CFI)**.

## The ergonomic problem

This project is an ergonomics story before it is a mechanics story. A
Daratumumab injection demands a pinch-grip force of roughly **3 kg (~30 N)**,
held steadily for three to five minutes — and oncology nurses repeat that up to
**five or more times a day**, day after day. That is a textbook recipe for
repetitive strain injury in the hand, wrist and forearm, and it also makes the
injection _worse for the patient_: a tiring hand delivers an uneven flow. We
measured the job before designing for it, and the measurement makes the
problem visible:

A flow model (Hagen–Poiseuille, validated against a water test to within half
a second) confirmed the target: the device had to replace a steady 30 N —
comfortably inside a 5–50 N envelope depending on tissue back-pressure.

## Designing with the clinic, not for it

From a morphological analysis, two concepts were prototyped and taken to
**Reinier de Graaf Hospital**: an elastic assist ("Elastico") that supplements
finger force, and the **CFI**, in which a pre-tensioned constant-force spring
drives the plunger autonomously. The clinicians' verdict was the real design
review — the elastic concept was uncomfortable in exactly the way the current
process is, while the CFI **removed the effort altogether** while keeping the
nurse in control: tension the spring with a lever, clamp the standard
BD Luer-Lock syringe, insert the needle, release. Manual override remains
possible throughout.

<Split src={cfiImprovements} alt="The constant-force injector prototype with red annotations marking redesigned regions: the spring drum rim, the syringe clamp area and the trigger block on the housing side." caption="Every annotated change came from clinical or bench feedback: click-and-twist syringe clamping, a spring-loaded hook replacing the fiddly pin, and graduation marks for at-a-glance rate feedback." reverse>

The refinements are ergonomics too, not detailing: the hospital asked for a
button instead of a fiddly locking pin, so the pin became a **spring-loaded
hook** that locks itself when tensioned; the syringe holder gained a
**click-and-twist** clamp so the syringe cannot eject under load; and
graduation marks along the shaft give the nurse the progress feedback
clinicians explicitly requested — confirmation, at a glance, that the
injection is running at constant speed. Everything except the spring prints in
PLA, oriented so peak loads run perpendicular to the print layers.

## Validation — honest numbers

The prototype's repeatability was its strongest result: **five consecutive
trials produced nearly overlapping volume–time curves**, markedly more
consistent than the manual injections measured earlier. The spring delivers its
force linearly, cycle after cycle — the manufacturer rates it for 8,000
cycles, which at five injections a day is over four years of service.

The duration test is where honesty matters. Against a 3–5 minute target, the
CFI delivered the 15 mL surrogate in **7 minutes 55 seconds**. A water-only
null test isolated the causes: internal friction in the drum-and-shaft
mechanism eats a measurable share of the spring force (45 s measured vs ~30 s
theoretical), and the actual viscosity of Daratumumab is — remarkably —
**unavailable**: neither the manufacturer nor the hospital pharmacy could
provide it, so our surrogate was calibrated to an estimate. The device works;
its spring must be re-specified against the real drug.

## The regulatory pathway — as much a design input as the spring

A device that touches chemotherapy administration lives or dies by the EU
**Medical Device Regulation**, so the regulatory strategy was designed
alongside the mechanism, not after it. The CFI deliberately targets the
**Article 5.5 in-house exemption**: manufactured and used within the same
hospital, it avoids the full commercial CE-marking route — but the exemption
is not a shortcut. It still binds the hospital to the MDR's general safety and
performance requirements, technical documentation, **post-market surveillance
and vigilance**, and it comes with hard restrictions: the device may never be
transferred to another legal entity, and the exemption only holds while no
equivalent commercial device is available.

Those rules shaped the hardware directly. Reusability implies repeated
**70% ethanol sterilisation** — and porous PLA measurably degrades under it,
capping the printed housing's service life at roughly one to three years
against the spring's four-plus. The honest consequence: as designed, the CFI
is an annually reprinted device (about €2 of material and 12 hours of print
time), and moving to resin printing or sealed coatings is the first
compliance-driven follow-up, not an aesthetic one. Complex internal geometry
that is hard to disinfect thoroughly was flagged as a genuine contamination
risk in the same analysis.

## Lessons learned

- **Clinical feedback beats desk criteria.** The concept that scored well on
  paper was rejected in minutes by the people who would use it — and the best
  design changes (hook, clamp, progress marks) were all clinician requests.
- **Regulation is a design input, not paperwork.** The MDR Article 5.5 route
  and its sterilisation demands set the material constraints as firmly as any
  load case.
- **Measure the task before designing for it.** The 30 N figure anchored every
  downstream decision, from spring selection to FEM load cases.
- **Missing data is a finding.** That a drug's viscosity is unavailable even to
  the hospital pharmacy defines the next iteration's first experiment — and
  explains the honest 7:55 vs 3–5 min gap.
