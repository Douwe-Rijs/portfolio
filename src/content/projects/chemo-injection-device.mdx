## Overview

Daratumumab (Darzalex), a monoclonal antibody used against multiple myeloma, is
injected subcutaneously: 15–20 mL pushed by hand over **3–5 minutes of
continuous pressure**. Nurses administering it several times a day report
serious strain in hand, wrist and forearm. For our Bachelor End Project at
TU Delft — in collaboration with **Reinier de Graaf Hospital** — our
four-person team designed, built and validated a device that takes that load
off the caregiver entirely: a 3D-printed **Constant Force Injector (CFI)**.

## The problem, quantified

Before designing anything, we measured the job. A test rig — syringe, scale,
collection bag and a honey–water mixture matched to the drug's estimated
viscosity — showed a nurse must sustain roughly **3 kg (~30 N)** of plunger
force to hit the target flow rate, for minutes at a time.

The physics backed the measurement: modelling the needle flow with the
**Hagen–Poiseuille equation** put the required force in the 5–50 N range
depending on tissue back-pressure, and a water-only validation run landed
within half a second of theory (30.8 s measured vs 30.3 s predicted).

## Design decisions

From a morphological analysis, two concepts were prototyped and taken to the
hospital: an elastic assist ("Elastico") that supplements finger force, and the
**CFI**, in which a pre-tensioned **constant-force spring** drives the plunger
autonomously. Clinical feedback was decisive — the elastic concept was
uncomfortable in exactly the way the current process is, while the CFI removed
the effort altogether. The workflow: tension the spring with a lever, clamp the
standard BD Luer-Lock syringe, insert the needle, release.

<Split src={cfiImprovements} alt="The constant-force injector prototype with red annotations marking redesigned regions: the spring drum rim, the syringe clamp area and the trigger block on the housing side." caption="Clinical and bench feedback drove the redesign: click-and-twist syringe clamping, a spring-loaded hook replacing the fiddly pin, and graduation marks for visual rate feedback." reverse>

The refinement round is where the design matured: the syringe holder gained a
**click-and-twist** clamp so the syringe cannot eject under load, the manual
locking pin became a **spring-loaded hook** that locks automatically when
tensioned, and graduation marks along the shaft let the nurse verify at a
glance that the injection is proceeding at constant speed. Every part except
the spring prints in PLA, with each component oriented so its maximum load runs
perpendicular to the print layers.

</Split>

## Validation — honest numbers

The prototype's repeatability was its strongest result: **five consecutive
trials produced nearly overlapping volume–time curves**, markedly more
consistent than the manual injections measured earlier. The spring delivers its
force linearly, cycle after cycle — the manufacturer rates it for 8,000
cycles, which at five injections a day is over four years of service.

The duration test is where honesty matters. Against a 3–5 minute target, the
CFI delivered the 15 mL surrogate in **7 minutes 55 seconds**. A water-only
null test isolated the causes: internal friction in the drum-and-shaft
mechanism eats a measurable share of the spring force (45 s measured vs ~30 s
theoretical), and the actual viscosity of Daratumumab is — remarkably —
**unavailable**: neither the manufacturer nor the hospital pharmacy could
provide it, so our surrogate was calibrated to an estimate. The device works;
its spring must be re-specified against the real drug.

## Regulatory path

As a device intended for in-house hospital use, the CFI targets the **MDR
Article 5.5** exemption — with its documentation, safety and post-market
surveillance duties — rather than a commercial CE route. Sterilisation is the
honest weak point: porous PLA degrades under repeated 70% ethanol cleaning,
which caps the printed housing's life at roughly one to three years (about €2
of material and 12 hours of printing to replace) and motivates resin printing
or coatings as follow-up work.

## Lessons learned

- **Measure the task before designing for it.** The 30 N figure anchored every
  downstream decision, from spring selection to FEM load cases.
- **Clinical feedback beats desk criteria.** The concept that scored well on
  paper was rejected in minutes by the people who would use it.
- **Friction is a first-class design load.** The gap between theoretical and
  actual delivery time was mostly mechanism friction — predictable in
  hindsight, measured too late.
- **Missing data is a finding.** That a drug's viscosity is unavailable even to
  the hospital pharmacy defines the next iteration's first experiment.
